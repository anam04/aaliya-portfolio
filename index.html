Hi, I'm Aaliya!

I'm a PhD student in the Department of Biobehavioral Health at Pennsylvania State University, where I'm fortunate to work with Dr. Idan Shalev.

My research focuses on how early-life adversity shapes stress pathways and accelerates biological aging. I study telomere biology, epigenetic clocks, HPA-axis dysregulation, and oxidative stress — trying to understand how childhood experiences get "under the skin" at the cellular level.

I have previously conducted research at Nanyang Technological University (Singapore), the University of Alabama at Birmingham, and Shiv Nadar University. You can learn more about these projects here.

I'm originally from India. In my free time, I enjoy reading, listening to music, and taking photographs.

Penn State (2024–Present)

I'm currently a first-year PhD student in Biobehavioral Health at Penn State, working with Dr. Idan Shalev. My dissertation research investigates how early-life adversity — particularly childhood maltreatment — becomes biologically embedded through multiple stress pathways.

Specifically, I'm examining whether HPA-axis dysregulation and oxidative stress mediate the relationship between child maltreatment and telomere length. Telomeres are protective caps on our chromosomes; shorter telomeres are a marker of accelerated cellular aging. The idea that how you were treated as a child might literally shorten your cells is both scientifically fascinating and deeply important for understanding health disparities.

I'm also collaborating on a project examining reproductive profiles and epigenetic aging in post-menopausal women — asking whether hormonal history leaves a signature in the epigenome. This project is currently under review.

[Fun Stuff] — Research keywords I find myself using a lot
  • Epigenetic clocks — algorithmic estimates of biological age from DNA methylation patterns (PCPhenoAge, PCGrimAge, DunedinPACE)
  • Telomere length (DNAmTL) — a hallmark of cellular senescence and stress exposure
  • HPA-axis — the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal stress response system
  • Oxidative stress — cellular damage from reactive oxygen species, a downstream consequence of chronic stress

NTU Singapore (Jan–Jun 2024)

I was selected for the India Connect Fall Research Fellowship at Nanyang Technological University, Singapore — a competitive program that funds Indian students to conduct research in Singapore. My final-year project there involved molecular biology work that deepened my wet-lab skills and gave me a wonderful first experience working internationally.

Living in Singapore for six months was formative in ways that go beyond the science. I came back with a much sharper sense of what kind of researcher I want to be.

University of Alabama at Birmingham (Summer 2023)

I spent a summer at UAB exploring both computational and experimental approaches to understanding cellular stress responses. Working across methods — at the bench and at the computer — confirmed my belief that the best biology research requires both.

University of Delhi (Summer 2022)

My first formal research internship, in biological sciences, where I got my hands dirty with basic molecular mechanisms. I learned that research is mostly troubleshooting, and that troubleshooting is actually the interesting part.
